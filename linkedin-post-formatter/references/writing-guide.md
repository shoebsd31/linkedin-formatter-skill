# LinkedIn Post Writing Guide

Read this when you want depth on crafting the copy. The core rules live in
SKILL.md; this expands the "why" and gives patterns and examples.

## The fold is everything

LinkedIn collapses posts after ~2–3 lines (≈140–210 characters on desktop,
fewer on mobile) behind a "…more" link. Only readers who tap "…more" see the
rest, and the feed algorithm rewards dwell time and that expand click. So the
first two lines must earn the click on their own.

Strong hook patterns:
- **A surprising claim or result:** "We deleted 80% of our code and shipped
  faster."
- **A specific number/stat:** "Our onboarding took 47 minutes. Now it takes 4."
- **A confession or contrarian take:** "I was wrong about remote work."
- **A short story opener:** "A customer emailed me at 2am. What they said
  changed our roadmap."
- **A direct question that names the reader's pain.**

Avoid: "I'm thrilled/excited/humbled to announce…", generic context-setting,
or burying the point in paragraph three.

## Structure templates

Pick one based on intent.

**1. Story → lesson (most engaging)**
```
[Hook: a moment or tension]
[2–4 short beats of the story, one per line block]
[The turn / what changed]
[The lesson, generalized for the reader]
[CTA or question]
```

**2. Listicle / how-to**
```
[Hook naming the outcome]
[One line of setup]
→ point one
→ point two
→ point three
[Wrap-up + CTA]
```

**3. Announcement**
```
[Hook: the news as a benefit, not "we are pleased to announce"]
[What it is, in one line]
[Why it matters to the reader]
[1–3 concrete details or links]
[CTA: try it / read more / comment]
```

## Formatting for the eye

- **White space is your friend.** Blank lines between every 1–3 lines. The post
  should look airy in the feed.
- **Front-load each line.** People skim the left edge; put the meaningful word
  first.
- **Pseudo-bullets:** start lines with `→`, `•`, `—`, or `✅` then a space.
  LinkedIn has no list formatting, so these are just leading characters.
- **Bold (Unicode) for mini-headings or 2–4 key phrases**, never for whole
  paragraphs. Overuse looks like spam and breaks screen readers.
- **One emoji as a section marker** can aid scanning; a wall of emoji repels.

## Engagement mechanics

- **End with a question or low-friction CTA.** Comments weigh more than likes;
  asking "What's worked for you?" invites replies.
- **Reply to early comments fast** — momentum in the first hour matters. (Tell
  the user; the skill can't do this for them.)
- **Hashtags:** 3–5, specific over generic. `#ProductManagement` beats `#tech`.
  Put them at the very end so they don't break the reading flow.
- **Links and reach:** LinkedIn historically suppresses posts with outbound
  links in the body. Two common tactics: (a) put the link on its own line and
  accept it, or (b) say "link in the comments" and drop the URL in the first
  comment. Offer this; respect the user's choice.

## Tone

- Write like a person talking, not a press release. Short sentences. Contractions.
- Specific beats abstract: real numbers, names, and moments.
- One idea per post. If you have three ideas, that's three posts.
- Read it aloud. If a line is hard to say, cut it.

## Length

Most high-performing posts land between ~1300 and ~2000 characters — enough to
tell a story, short enough to finish. The hard cap is 3000; the script flags
anything over. Shorter punchy posts (under 800) also work for a single sharp
insight. Let the idea set the length, then cut 10%.

## Quick checklist before formatting

- [ ] First line works as a standalone hook (no setup, no "excited to share")
- [ ] Plenty of white space; no paragraph longer than ~3 lines
- [ ] Bold used on only a few key phrases, not everywhere
- [ ] Ends with a question or CTA
- [ ] 3–5 specific hashtags at the end
- [ ] Mentions written as `@[Full Name]`
- [ ] Under 3000 characters (the script confirms)
